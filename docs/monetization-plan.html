01 Product context & goal

What the product does and what the billing model must achieve.

Core workflow

  • User pastes a job description and their resume.
  • A workflow runs a reviewer → writer → reviewer loop until the reviewer is satisfied.
  • User can give feedback; the writer revises. User can tag the reviewer for a final pass.
  • When satisfied: pick a template, preview, download as PDF or Word.

The billing model must

Goals
  • Support a lower (~$6) and higher (~$10) tier.
  • Control inference cost per user.
  • Let Pro users reach stronger models.
  • Allow overage / top-up credits.
Constraints
  • Expensive users must not make the product loss-making.
  • Billing must not feel restrictive / hurt engagement.
  • Predictable resets; simple user-facing plans.

Making credits feel like value, not a meter

You flagged two things: credits feel like a meter, and the model→credit mapping should be less punitive. Three levers, drawn partly from how Lovable presents usage (small fractional numbers, cost shown subtly on hover, soft estimates):

1
Anchor on outcomes, not raw numbers. Lead the UI with "~12 tailorings left this month" (derived from average run cost) and keep the raw credit number secondary. Users think in resumes; credits stay the accounting layer.
2
Soften the model multiplier. Don't pass the full provider price delta to the user. A premium model that costs 6× at the API might be shown as only ~2–3× in credits — you absorb part of the spread so picking a better model doesn't feel like a punishment. Margin is protected by gating + caps, not by scary per-run pricing.
3
Show cost calmly, after the fact. No live countdown during a run. Show "this run used ~X credits" once it completes (hover for the breakdown), like Lovable's per-message cost on hover. A countdown timer is what creates anxiety.
Lean: "Tailoring credits" as the unit, but the primary surface is an estimated "runs remaining" figure. Absorb part of the model premium so Smart/Premium feels like a small step up, not a penalty.

Internal debit formula

Should a subscription credit cost the same as an overage credit?

Your instinct: no — we want subscriptions to be the better deal, not credit-buying. Two distinct levers do that, and they're not mutually exclusive:

Lever A
Access gating (who can buy). Standalone credits are not sold to non-subscribers at all. Overage is a subscriber-only convenience. The subscription is the membership; you can't buy your way in with credits alone.
Lever B
Price gating (what a credit costs). A credit included in a subscription is cheaper per credit than a credit bought as overage. The subscription's bundled credits are the headline value; overage carries a modest premium.

Three combinations to choose between:

Model 1
Gating only, parity pricing. 1 sub credit = 1 overage credit in value, but overage is subscriber-only. Simple; subscription wins purely because non-subscribers get nothing. (This is the "lock buying to subscribers" idea from your note.)
Model 2
Pricing only. Anyone can buy credits, but subscription credits are meaningfully cheaper per credit. Risk: lets heavy users avoid subscribing if overage is close enough in price.
Model 3
Both (gating + premium). Overage is subscriber-only and priced at a premium per credit vs. the subscription's bundled rate. Strongest pull toward subscribing.
Lean: Model 3. Overage stays subscriber-only (matches your abuse note) and carries a small premium, so the subscription is always the best value-per-credit. The exact premium % is a "how much" question → settle it after cost data lands.
↳ Decide — which model (1 / 2 / 3)? Structure only; % later.

Top-up caps, rollover, and the cancellation edge case

You set three rules: cap top-ups per tier, roll unused overage forward as overage (never fold into the subscription), and only allow overage while subscribed. Mechanics to nail down:

Top-up caps per tier

TierTop-up ceilingRationale
Basic≤ 2× monthly allowanceHeavy Basic users feel the ceiling → natural nudge to Pro.
Pro≤ 3–4× monthly allowanceMore headroom for genuine power users without uncapped exposure.

"Ceiling" = max outstanding overage balance you can hold at once, not a per-purchase limit — so they can't stockpile $50 and bail.

Rollover

Unused overage moves into next cycle as a separate overage pool (6-month expiry from purchase, per §8). It never merges with subscription credits. Two distinct buckets in the ledger: subscriptionCredits (expire end-of-cycle) and overageCredits (expire +6mo). Clean accounting, matches your "don't fold it in" rule.

The cancellation edge case ⚠️

Your rule: cancel → overage inaccessible. One caution — permanently forfeiting already-paid-for credits on cancel can feel hostile and may raise consumer-law questions in some jurisdictions. A softer version keeps the abuse protection without the bad taste:

A
Forfeit on cancel. Strongest anti-abuse, but riskiest on goodwill / refunds.
B
Freeze on cancel, restore on resubscribe. Overage is locked while there's no active subscription, but comes back (until its 6-month expiry) if they return. Same practical effect — you can't use overage without paying for a subscription — but nothing is "stolen".
Lean: Option B (freeze, not forfeit). Achieves your anti-abuse goal — overage is useless without an active subscription — while avoiding the "you took my money" reaction. Overage still hard-expires at 6 months regardless.
↳ Decide — top-up ceilings + forfeit vs. freeze on cancel?

Can overage unlock better models than your tier? (credit-pool selection)

This is the thread running through your §6, §7 and §11 notes: let a user choose which pool funds a run, so a Basic user could spend overage to reach a model their subscription tier normally can't. It's powerful but it can quietly cannibalise the Pro upsell. Three positions:

A
Strict tier gating. Model class depends only on subscription tier; overage just buys more of the same classes. Simplest, cleanest upsell — Basic stays Basic. But it contradicts your "purchased credits can access more premium models" instinct.
B
Overage unlocks one class up, capped. Basic's overage can reach Smart (not Premium); Pro's overage can reach the top non-blocked class. User picks the pool per run. Ultra stays blocked for everyone. Gives flexibility without collapsing the ladder.
C
Overage unlocks everything non-blocked. Any subscriber with overage reaches Premium. Maximum flexibility, but now "why pay for Pro?" — the answer must be the other Pro perks (history, templates, loop caps, included Smart/Premium allowance), not model access.
Lean: Option B. It honours your instinct (purchased credits reach further) without gutting Pro, because Pro still gets Smart/Premium included and one-class-higher reach via its own overage. Critically, your real Pro moat is the §5 list — history, premium templates, higher loop/message limits — so model access alone shouldn't have to carry the upsell.
↳ Decide — strict gating (A), one-class-up via overage (B), or full unlock (C)?

What Lovable does — and a less anxious reservation model

How Lovable handles it (checked their docs): they do not show a scary "max" or reserve-and-refund. They charge the actual complexity-based cost after the action, in small fractional numbers (a style tweak ≈ 0.5, a bigger build ≈ 1.7), and you only see the per-message cost if you hover. Monthly credits expire ~2 months out; top-ups last 12 months; daily grants don't roll over. The calm comes from small numbers, shown quietly, after the fact — not from a pre-flight quote.

Two ways to run our looping workflow safely:

A
Visible reservation (original §13 plan). Show "Estimated 80–160, max 200", hold the max, refund the unused. Maximally transparent, but flashing a big "max 200" upfront is exactly the anxiety trigger you want to avoid.
B
Quiet reservation (Lovable-style). Internally still reserve the max so the loop can't overrun your balance — but the user only sees a soft "this usually costs ~X" and, after completion, "used X credits". You only block a run if the balance can't cover the minimum estimate (then offer Fast mode or overage). No countdown, no scary ceiling.
Lean: Option B. Keep the reservation as an internal guardrail (protects margin on the loop) but present it like Lovable — small estimate before, actual cost after, details on hover. Reserve the safety, hide the scaffolding.
↳ Decide — quiet reservation (B), or show the full estimate/max (A)?

Dynamic loop caps: base 3, modulated by tier · model · balance

You want a base of 3 iterations for everyone, more for higher tiers, adjusted by the selected model and remaining credits. A simple formula keeps it predictable:

effectiveCap = min( tierCap, modelCap, floor(creditsRemaining ÷ perLoopCost) )
FactorEffect on cap
TierBasic 3 · Pro 4–5 (your §5 "higher message limits" perk).
Model classPricier class → lower cap, so one run can't drain the balance (e.g. Premium capped at 2–3 even for Pro).
Credits leftNever start a loop you can't afford to finish; if balance only covers 2 of 4 loops, cap at 2 and surface "add credits / switch model for more passes".

The reviewer can still converge early (satisfied before the cap) — the cap is just the ceiling, not a target. Most runs should finish under it.

Lean: Base 3 (Basic), Pro 5, with a per-model override that lowers the cap for Premium, and a hard "can you afford the next loop?" check before each iteration. Tune the exact numbers once cost-per-loop data lands.

Do we still need an input-length cap?

You asked: why cap input length when longer inputs just cost more credits? You're mostly right — credits already meter length, so no arbitrary low cap is needed. But two non-billing reasons remain:

1
Model context windows are a hard technical ceiling. A genuinely huge paste can exceed the model's input window and the run simply fails — better to catch that before charging anything. This is a validation limit, not a pricing limit.
2
One accidental run shouldn't quietly cost a fortune. Someone pasting a 200-page PDF could burn a large chunk of their balance in a single click. Not a hard cap — just a "this input is large and will cost ~X credits, continue?" confirmation above a threshold.
Lean: No arbitrary length cap (you're right — credits handle it). Instead: (a) a technical max tied to the chosen model's context window, and (b) a soft "large input → cost preview & confirm" prompt. Aligns with §11's quiet-reservation approach.
↳ Decide — no cap + context-window validation + large-input confirm?

18 Internal billing architecture

Three balances per user

1) Monthly subscription credits · 2) Promotional credits · 3) Purchased credits.

Spend priority — expiresFirst

Consume the credits whose expiry is nearest, first. Since subscription credits expire end-of-cycle and overage expires +6 months, this naturally spends subscription credits before overage — no separate rule needed, and it minimises wasted (expired) credits for the user.

Override: if §9's pool-selection lands (overage unlocks a higher model class), a premium run can explicitly draw from the overage pool even when subscription credits remain. So: expiresFirst by default, pool-choice when the user deliberately picks a gated model.

Per-workflow usage record

user · plan · workflow id · mode · model · est min/max · reserved · final charged · actual provider cost · markup · input/output tokens · reviewer/writer/revision counts · export count · status · created date.

Positioning the bundle (secondary option) honestly

You want the one-off bundle to exist, be priced differently, not be the obvious choice, and carry expiry — without dark patterns. The honest way to make subscription the better deal is on value, not trickery:

1
Price per credit higher than the subscription. A bundle is convenience for someone who refuses recurring billing, so it legitimately costs more per credit. Subscription wins on math, transparently — no hidden cost, the difference is shown plainly.
2
Give it a clear expiry. e.g. valid 30–60 days — it matches a burst of job applications and bounds your liability. State it up front.
3
Discoverable, not default. Subscription is the primary CTA; the bundle is a visible secondary link ("just need a one-off? → buy a pack"). Not buried (that would be a dark pattern), not competing for the headline.

The honest-nudge line: showing "subscription = better value per credit" is fine; hiding the bundle, pre-checking the pricier option, or making cancellation hard is not. Stay on the right side of that.

Lean: Offer the bundle as a clearly-labelled secondary option with a higher per-credit price and a 30–60 day expiry. Lower priority for v1 — ship subscription + overage first, add bundles once the core loop is proven.

🔒 Locked — build against these

Decided and stable. Safe to design and implement now.

  • No free / trial tier. Account + email verification (Clerk → Google/Apple). You must subscribe to use the product. Rate-limiting is effectively the credit balance.
  • Two tiers at launch — Basic & Pro. Monthly billing only for now. Prices are still movable anchors; annual + an optional Power tier come later, data-permitting.
  • Credits are the unit ("tailoring credits"). Outcome-anchored UI: lead with "~N runs left", raw credits secondary, run cost shown calmly on hover after the run (Lovable-style). Absorb part of the model premium so better models don't feel punitive.
  • Credit lifecycle. Subscription credits expire each billing cycle (no rollover). Purchased overage credits roll forward and expire in ~6 months. Consumption order is expiresFirst.
  • Overage / top-ups. Only purchasable & usable with an active subscription, within the billing cycle. Per-tier purchase caps (higher tiers unlock larger packs). Freeze on cancel, restore on resubscribe — no forfeiture.
  • Pricing parity (Model 1). A subscription credit and an overage credit carry the same value — gate by access, don't surcharge overage. Flagged risk: overage reaching better models can cannibalise higher tiers; that's what the caps below defend.
  • Model gating by class. Economy → Standard → Smart → Premium. Ultra (Fable, gpt-5.5 pro class) not served for now; no BYOK yet. Gate by subscription tier; overage may reach one class up, capped — a taste, not the full entree. Model names visible to all Advanced-mode users (never paywalled).
  • Loop caps. Base 3 (Basic), Pro 5. Per-model override can lower the cap for Premium models; hard "can you afford the next loop?" check before each iteration.
  • Input length. No flat cap — validate per-model against the context window, and warn before a very large request (Claude Code-style confirm).
  • Weekly pacing. Visible, but in a usage-review view — not surfaced mid-run.
  • Reservation. Quiet reservation — reserve internally for safety, present softly. No scary pre-charge or live countdown.
